It's important you know...

Parents Know Their Child Best

Autism signs usually emerge gradually, and are noticeable before age three. Knowing this, parents are the ones uniquely positioned to observe, interact, and recognize signs of autism far earlier than diagnosed with their children. So, parents should investigate and monitor the development of their child if they are not hitting key milestones. Early concerns should be checked out and not dismissed as “just delays”.

In fact, the Canadian Pediatric Society recommends screening for all children before the age of three.











Here are some...

Possible Red Flags (any age less than 3)

Knowing what to look for by recognizing the early signs of autism or a probable neurodevelopmental disorder can lead to ideal outcomes. Keeping this in mind, it is important for parents to recognize that not all children will demonstrate these symptoms. According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, the early signs of autism spectrum disorder in young children include:






Name Responding


Not responding to his/her name by 12 months of age.





Objects of Interest


Not pointing at objects to show interest by 14 months.





Playing Pretend


Not playing "pretend" games by 18 months.





Eye-Contact


Avoiding eye contact or preferring to be alone.





Sound Response


Seems not to respond to sound and may appear deaf.





Developmental Regressions


May regress in development such as losing language and/or proficiency skills.





Minor Changes


Getting upset by minor changes.





Certain Behaviours


Flapping their hands, rocking their body, or spinning in circles.





Unusual Reactions


Having unusual and sometimes intense reactions to the way things smell, taste, feel and/or look










There are also...

Developmental "red flags" (any age less than 3)

A child showing these delays in development should be given access to an immediate screening and assessment by a qualified healthcare professional:






Warm Expressions


No big smiles or other warm, joyful expressions by 6 months.





Sharing Facial Expressions


No back-and-forth sharing of sounds, smiles, or other facial expressions by 9 months.





Communications


No babbling, lack of response to name and no back-and-forth gestures by 12 months.





Speaking


No spoken words by 16 months.





Repeating Phrases


No imitation or repeating of meaningful two-word phrase by 24 months.





Developmental Regressions


Regressions in development such as losing language and/or proficiency skills.










Don't forget about...

Possible Signs of Autism at Any Age

This is where it's crucial to note that each child develops at their own pace. However, if your child is not meeting the milestones for his or her age, or you suspect a problem, no matter the age, it is important for parents to bring their concerns to a healthcare professional share your concerns with your child’s doctor immediately. Possible signs of autism at any age include:






Eye-Contact


Avoiding eye contact or preferring to be alone.





Understanding Feelings


Struggles with understanding other people’s feelings.





Language Deficiencies


Remains nonverbal or has delayed language development.





Echolalia


Repeats words or phrases over and over.





Minor Changes


Gets upset by minor changes in routine or surroundings.





Limited Interests


Has highly restricted interests.





Repetitive Behaviours


Performs repetitive behaviors such as flapping, rocking or spinning.





Unusual Reactions


Has unusual and often intense reactions to sounds, smells, tastes, textures, lights and/or colors.










Make sure you remember...

Recommended Actions For Parents

It's important that parents know which actions they have to take once they start to recognize certain red flags. Make sure youtrust your instincts, monitor your child’s development, arrange for a visit with healthcare provider, request for an assessment and a formal diagnosis by a developmental specialist, and seek a second opinion if necessary.
