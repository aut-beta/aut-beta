ASD Diagnosis

It's quite difficult to diagnose autism spectrum disorder (ASD), since there's no medical test like a blood test to diagnose autism. However, specially trained physicians or psychologists can look at the child's developmental history and conduct behavioral evaluations using professional assessment tools as well as rely on the observations of parents to learn about the child and make a diagnosis.

Diagnosis is made by evaluating three behavioral groups:






1. Social Interactions


The level of social interaction as your child interacts with peers and parents.





2. Verbal Interactions


The level of verbal interactions and difficulty in expressing their needs either by voicing or relying on other forms of communication.





3. Repetitive Behaviours


The level of repetitive behaviors and narrow field of interests unique from others.

Early Diagnosis and Intervention

Autism diagnosis can be made reliably by age two, although many children do not receive diagnosis till they are much older. Delay in diagnosis results in missed opportunities to access early intervention to improve outcomes. According to Carmosino (2020), early behavioral intervention helps keep negative behaviors from becoming established and persistent as the child continues to grow.

Starting individualized therapy equips and helps the child develop skill sets that will enable easier integration and socialization in a group setting such as the school.

Overview of the Diagnostic Assessment

The Canadian Academy of Paediatricians recommends that all children be screened for developmental delays at their 9, 18, 24 or 30-month well-child visits and specifically for autism at their 18 and 24-month well-child visits . More screening is recommended for children with high risk for developmental problems and autism spectrum disorders such as those with older parents, have a family member with ASD, or born with low birth weight.

A. Before the Assessment
1. Prepare for the doctor’s appointment | Have a copy of your child’s development based on milestone checklists, and notes detailing your child behaviour including when it started, the frequency of occurrence, triggers, and time of the day it happens most. You can also access and complete any screening tool such as M-CHAT test. Finally, schedule an appointment with your family physician as soon as possible.
2. Visit your family doctor | Your family physician will conduct a wellness check and perform a developmental screening. The doctor may ask parents questions on concerns and observations during the screening process including: how often your child behaves a certain way, when you first noticed symptoms, your child developmental milestones, your family’s medical history, and why you decided to seek help.
3. Get a referral to a specialist/diagnostic assessment | Your physician might refer your child to qualified professionals to conduct autism assessment. They include pediatricians, developmental pediatricians, psychiatrists, psychologist psychologists and psychological associates, and nurse practitioners. In Ontario, you can receive a diagnosis of autism from any of the five diagnostic hubs funded by the ministry of children, community and social services (MCCSS). The diagnostic hubs provide timely assessments for your child and help to provide multi-disciplinary diagnostic assessments, connect you with the appropriate services in your community following an assessment, and offer local training and resources for qualified professionals so you can get a diagnosis faster. You are not required to get a diagnosis of autism to apply to register for the Ontario Autism Program (OAP).

B. During the Assessment
Be prepared and ready for your child's assessment. The specialist will ask you questions about your child’s behaviour and development to compile the medical and developmental history. He/she will also: observe your child, review documents, reports and previous assessments, including information from a school or childcare provider, and use standardized professional assessment tools and criteria to determine a diagnosis, including the diagnostic criteria outlined in the fifth edition of the Diagnostic and Statistics Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM 5). The assessment might also be with a team of specialists experienced in diagnosing ASD such as a developmental pediatrician, a child psychologist, a neuropsychologist, and a speech pathologist. It may be that the child is diagnosed with a developmental delay requiring further testing to determine the specific issue.

C. After the Assessment
The specialist will meet with you to discuss the results and give you a written report. The formal assessment report may indicate that your child needs support with skill development and the type of support. The report must include the following: your child’s full name and date of birth, the date of your child’s assessment, a statement indicating the diagnostic criteria for ASD, and the qualified professional’s name and credentials. Other information that might be in your child’s report include: clinic name and location, type of diagnostic screening tool used, additional diagnoses, if applicable, your child’s level of ability and functioning, and recommendations for treatment or potential next steps.

D. Further Assessments and Referrals
Your child might need further assessments or additional testing depending on the child’s needs. These might include: hearing or audiological assessment by an audiologist to rule out hearing difficulties or problems, developmental assessment or cognitive evaluation by a psychologist to measure verbal and nonverbal activities using standardized tests, speech and language evaluation by a speech-language pathologist or psychologist for communication difficulties, and/or a sensorimotor skills evaluation by an occupational therapist for sensory difficulties and challenge.
